Study 01 | Maharashtra onion markets

Can water stress help explain onion price shocks?

Onion prices rise in ways people feel before they read a report. This study asks a careful public-data question: when rainfall is weak and groundwater is already stressed, do we later see changes in onion production, mandi arrivals, and modal prices?

The answer may be partial. Prices also move because of storage, transport, exports, imports, policy decisions, demand, and production in nearby regions. So Track & Tote will look for signals, test lags, and keep the uncertainty visible.

In plain words

We are testing a chain, not telling a shortcut story.

If rains are weak during crop-sensitive months, farmers may rely more on groundwater. If groundwater is already deep or slow to recover, crop output can become more fragile. Later, markets may receive fewer onions, and prices may react.

But a market price is never just one thing. The study will show what lines up in the data and what still needs another explanation.

How we keep it scientific

Readable for everyone, disciplined enough for data work.

The method starts simple: define the question, collect public sources, align them to the same geography and time unit, then check whether the signal survives basic tests.

  1. Ask first.Write the hypothesis before looking for a chart.
  2. Audit sources.Provider, license, update date, missing values, and limitations.
  3. Build one table.District x month x crop, with raw and derived variables kept separate.
  4. Check lags.Compare 1-month, 3-month, and 6-month relationships before claiming a pattern.
  5. Publish caveats.Every chart gets a note on what it may show and what it cannot prove.

Result language

The conclusion has to match the evidence.

What we can say early

Rainfall deficit and lower arrivals appear together in these districts and months.

What needs more testing

The relationship remains after checking groundwater, seasonality, nearby supply, and policy context.

What we should not say yet

Water stress caused the onion price increase.
